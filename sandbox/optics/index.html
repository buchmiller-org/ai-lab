OPTICS

Game design prototypes — corporate satire / stealth puzzle

Prototype A
Decision Engine — Oregon Trail loop →

Finding: Decisions felt weightless — if a meter dropped, you just picked the option that raised it. No tension. The trilemma concept is sound but needs physical or time constraints to bite. Better as a meta-layer between levels than a standalone mechanic.

Prototype B
Top-Down Office — free-roam stealth →

Finding: Immediately more alive than A — LOS-based danger creates real-time pressure. But without task urgency, there's no reason to take routing risks. Continuous slow meter drain is less dramatic than discrete events. Boss patrol being predictable was noted as a potential feature.

Prototype C
Puzzle Level — fixed patrol + timed tasks →

Finding: Visible patrol route + task deadlines moved it toward a puzzle feel. Closer to the right direction but not yet challenging or interesting enough. The open floor means the boss is always avoidable; task sequences weren't tight enough to force hard decisions.

Design Observations & Directions

What's not working (yet)

  • Open floor = easy avoidance. The office needs walls, cubicle clusters, and narrow corridors that force routing through dangerous zones. A smart LOS design does nothing if the player can always walk around it.
  • One slow boss = one safe path. A single predictable watcher always leaves a safe side. Two overlapping patrol cones that leave only brief windows is where actual dilemmas form.
  • Prototypes lacked a "no good option" moment. The best puzzle games force you to choose between two bad options. None of the three prototypes achieved this.

What is working

  • Discrete penalties beat continuous drain. The one-time Visibility hit on entering idle state (Prototype C) was more impactful than slow meter bleed. Punishment should feel like an event, not erosion.
  • The trilemma (W/O/V) has good bones. Wellbeing vs. Output vs. Visibility genuinely pull in different directions; the issue is execution, not concept.
  • The patrol path visual is a good decision. Showing the route explicitly makes it a planning game, not a reaction game — which fits the "puzzle" framing better.

Promising directions forward

  • Real office geometry. Cubicle walls, conference room glass, narrow hallways. The layout itself does the design work of creating chokepoints.
  • Two-boss choreography. Two patrollers with overlapping cones create brief safe windows that the player must exploit. Timing the crossing becomes the core skill.
  • Task interdependency. Require gathering two items before delivering (e.g., pick up the file AND the signature, then deliver). Adds route planning depth without adding new mechanics.
  • The meta-layer makes level modifiers, not just stat swings. Decision cards between levels should change the level itself — "VP doing floor walks" adds a second watcher, "All-hands today" blocks a corridor route — so decisions have spatial consequences.
  • Clocks not meters. Replace slow-draining wellbeing with a per-task time budget visible as a countdown. Fail a task = permanent scar (a warning marker visible in the review). Makes each moment legible.
  • Playtest the visual direction of "Hitman lite." The best reference might be early Hitman: a small space, a learnable patrol, and a goal that requires violating the patrol zone at least once. Office-themed.
